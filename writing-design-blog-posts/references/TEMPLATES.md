# Article templates

Four structural templates for different article types. Each template shows the expected sections, their purpose, approximate word allocation, and an example opening.

---

## Case study

**Target**: UX Collective (primary)
**Length**: 2,500–3,500 words (10–15 min read)
**Tone**: Narrative, reflective, honest about failures

### Structure

```
# [Sentence-case title describing the project and your role/learning]
*[Subtitle: one sentence framing the project challenge or insight]*

## Context
[1–2 paragraphs. When, where, who. Name the company/client/project, your role,
and the team. Set the stage with enough detail that the reader can picture it.]
~150 words

## The challenge
[What problem were you solving? What was broken or missing? Why did it matter?
Be specific — name the users, the constraints, the stakes.]
~200 words

## Discovery / Research
[What did you do first? Retrospectives, user interviews, benchmarking,
proto-personas? Share the methodology AND the surprising findings.
Include Fig.N images of affinity diagrams, personas, journey maps.]
~400 words

## Strategy / Information architecture
[How did you translate research into structure? What decisions did you make
about navigation, hierarchy, content organization? Show your reasoning.]
~400 words

## Design / Prototyping
[Walk through the design evolution. Wireframes → high-fi prototypes.
Explain the business logic behind components, not just the visual choices.
Include Fig.N images of key screens.]
~500 words

## Testing / Validation
[How did you test? Who participated? What tasks did you set?
Share the "think-aloud" moments and unexpected findings.
Be honest about what didn't work.]
~400 words

## Results / Impact
[Quantitative if available (task completion rates, engagement metrics).
Qualitative insights. What changed because of this work?]
~200 words

## What I learned
[Personal reflection. What would you do differently? What surprised you?
What principle will you carry to the next project?
Acknowledge collaborators by name.]
~250 words
```

### Example opening

> At the end of 2013 I was involved in the Galassia Ariosto project at Net7, a small design and development studio in Pisa where I work. The goal was to build a digital archive for one of the most important works of Italian Renaissance literature — but the existing version was a mess. Nobody used it, the search barely worked, and the scholars we'd built it for had given up and gone back to paper.

### Key rules for case studies
- Show **process**, not just outcomes
- Document the "sad path" — edge cases and failures are where the real design thinking lives
- Use Fig.N captions for all images
- Always explain "why" before showing "what"
- Include at least one moment of honest failure or surprise
- No emojis

## Opinion piece / essay

**Target**: UX Collective (primary)
**Length**: 1,600–2,500 words (7–10 min read)
**Tone**: Thesis-driven, specific, critically engaged

### Structure

```
# [Sentence-case title with a clear point of view — not just a topic]
*[Subtitle: the specific argument in one sentence]*

[HOOK: 1–2 paragraphs. Personal observation, provocation, or shared pain point.
Establish your stake in this topic. Why you, why now.]
~200 words

## [Section advancing claim 1]
[Your strongest argument. Support with a specific example from your work
or a named company/product. Link to evidence.]
~400 words

## [Section advancing claim 2]
[Second angle. Different type of evidence — maybe industry data,
a trend you've observed, or a counterexample.]
~400 words

## [Section advancing claim 3]
[Deepen the argument. This is where nuance lives — explore the grey areas.
What's the counterargument? Where does your thesis break down?]
~300 words

## [Section with practical implications]
[What should the reader do with this? Not generic advice —
specific, actionable, grounded in the argument you've made.]
~300 words

## [Closing]
[Return to the opening image or anecdote. Restate the thesis in evolved form.
End with a forward-looking thought or genuine question, not a summary.]
~200 words
```

### Example title formulations that work
- "Why AI is exposing design's craft crisis"
- "It's incredible how many bad user experiences are still out there in 2025"
- "AI is coming for our design jobs, but it can't touch taste"
- "The great design handoff is already happening"
- "What I learned about design maturity by watching my team fall apart"

### Key rules for opinion pieces
- **Lead with a specific thesis**, not a topic. "AI and design" is a topic. "AI is making designers lazy because it rewards speed over taste" is a thesis.
- Name real companies, products, or scenarios — not hypotheticals
- Acknowledge counterarguments — don't strawman
- The UX Collective values **nuance and critical thinking** above all. Explore grey areas, ask tough questions.
- Personal anecdote is the primary differentiator from AI-generated content
- No emojis

## Tutorial / methodology

**Target**: UX Collective or Bootcamp
**Length**: 1,600–2,100 words (7 min read)
**Tone**: Instructional, encouraging, practical

### Structure

```
# [Sentence-case title describing what the reader will learn to do]
*[Subtitle: the specific outcome or deliverable]*

[HOOK: 1–2 paragraphs. The real situation where you needed this method.
What problem were you solving? What constraints did you face?]
~150 words

## What you'll need
[Prerequisites, tools, materials. Keep it practical.
Use a bullet list with bold lead-in phrases.]
~100 words

## [Step/section 1: Setup or foundation]
[First phase of the method. Explain why this step matters.
Include screenshots, templates, or code blocks.]
~300 words

## [Step/section 2: Core process]
[The main method. Be specific — name the tools, show the settings,
share the template. Use numbered steps within sections if needed.]
~400 words

## [Step/section 3: Execution]
[Putting it into practice. Walk through a real example.
Include images of actual outputs, not stock photos.]
~400 words

## [Step/section 4: Analysis / what you'll find]
[How to interpret results. What to look for. Common pitfalls.]
~300 words

## Tips and recommendations
[Bullet list with bold lead-in phrases.
Practical tips from your experience using this method.]
~200 words

## [Warm closing]
[Encouraging summary. Link to a downloadable template or resource.
Invite the reader to try it and share results.]
~150 words
```

### Example opening

> Throughout 2016 we ran more than 30 user testing sessions across three universities in Italy — and not one of them happened in a proper UX lab. We couldn't afford one. So we built what I call a Portable Lab: a lightweight testing setup that fits in a backpack and can be deployed in any quiet room with a power outlet. Here's exactly how we did it, and the template I use to run sessions.

### Key rules for tutorials
- **Show your actual setup** — photos of your desk, screenshots of your tools
- Share templates, spreadsheets, downloadable resources
- Use "you" address — you're teaching a peer
- Number steps when sequence matters
- Include a "what you'll need" section
- No emojis (unless career-adjacent content)

## Career guide / guidelines

**Target**: Bootcamp (primary), occasionally UX Collective
**Length**: 1,000–1,800 words (4–7 min read)
**Tone**: Casual, peer-to-peer, encouraging

### Structure

```
# [Sentence-case title — direct and helpful]
*[Subtitle: who this is for and what they'll get]*

[HOOK: 1–2 paragraphs. Your personal career story that led to this advice.
Be specific about the struggle.]
~150 words

## [Topic area 1]
[3–5 bullets with bold lead-in phrases. Each tip is specific and actionable.
Include a tool recommendation or link where relevant.]
~250 words

## [Topic area 2]
[Same format. More tips organized by theme.]
~250 words

## [Topic area 3]
[Same format.]
~250 words

## Tools and resources
[Curated list of specific tools, links, templates.
Bold the tool name, then explain what it's good for.]
~150 words

## [Warm closing]
[Encouraging message. "I hope this helps."
Name someone whose advice influenced you. Invite connection.]
~100 words
```

### Example opening

> Throughout 2022, I was on the hunt for a new job in Product Design. I applied to more than 50 positions, got rejected from most of them, made it to final rounds at a few, and eventually landed a role that turned out to be perfect. Along the way I built a system for organizing my applications, preparing for interviews, and keeping my portfolio sharp. Here's everything I learned.

### Key rules for career guides
- Emojis allowed sparingly (🤖🚀🌟) — max 3–5 per article
- Bullet-list dominant format
- Bold lead-in phrases are essential for scannability
- Single-sentence paragraphs are fine
- Be honest about rejection and difficulty — vulnerability resonates
- Always share at least one concrete resource (template, tool, spreadsheet)
